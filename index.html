5 Best Roofing Contractors
in Moncton, NB (2026)

How to find a licensed, insured roofer in Moncton — and what to look for before you sign anything.

Finding a good roofer in Moncton is harder than it should be. A quick Google search turns up dozens of names, but most homeowners have no idea who's actually licensed, who carries liability insurance, and who's going to show up when they say they will. This guide walks you through exactly what to look for — and how to get competing quotes without the runaround.

What Makes a Good Roofer in Moncton?

New Brunswick doesn't have a single unified roofing contractor license — but legitimate roofers will carry WorkSafeNB coverage and liability insurance of at least $2 million. Always ask for both before they set foot on your roof. Any professional will provide this without hesitation. If they can't, move on.

The 5 Things to Check Before Hiring

1. Proof of Liability Insurance

Ask them to email you a current certificate of insurance naming you as additionally insured for the duration of the job. This protects you if a worker is injured on your property or if they cause damage.

2. WorkSafeNB Clearance Letter

Ask for a current clearance letter from WorkSafeNB. This confirms they are registered and in good standing. Takes 2 minutes for them to pull — if they refuse, they likely aren't registered.

3. Written Quote with Materials Specified

A written quote should specify the shingle brand and grade (e.g. IKO Cambridge, GAF Timberline), the number of layers being removed, whether ice and water shield is included on the eaves, and the warranty terms.

4. Local References

Ask for 2–3 recent jobs in Greater Moncton you can call. A contractor who just finished a roof in Riverview last month is a much safer bet than someone driving in from out of town.

5. Payment Terms — Never 100% Upfront

A reasonable payment structure is 10–20% deposit, 40% at material delivery, and the remainder on completion. Any contractor demanding full payment upfront before work starts is a red flag.

NB Power offers up to $2,000 back on qualifying heat pump installs. Here's exactly how to claim it.

Heat pumps are the single best home upgrade you can make in Atlantic Canada right now — and both New Brunswick and Nova Scotia are paying you to do it. Here's the current state of the rebate programs and exactly how to claim your money.

NB Power customers can receive rebates for qualifying heat pump installations. As of 2026, the rebate amounts are:

System TypeRebate AmountRequirements
Mini-split (single zone)Up to $500Must be ENERGY STAR certified

Efficiency Nova Scotia Rebates

Federal Greener Homes Grant

The Canada Greener Homes Grant program has evolved in 2025–2026. Check canada.ca/greener-homes for the most current federal rebate available alongside provincial programs — stacking both federal and provincial rebates is allowed and can significantly reduce your cost.

Every homeowner in Moncton has a story about a contractor who said they'd call back and never did. It's frustrating — but understanding why it happens makes it easy to avoid. Here's the full picture on getting quotes that actually result in someone showing up.

Why Contractors Don't Call Back

Good contractors in Moncton and Halifax are genuinely busy — especially from May through October. When they receive a vague inquiry like "hey what would it cost to redo my kitchen?" they often don't prioritize it because they have no idea if you're serious. The solution is simple: be specific and signal that you're ready to move.

✅ What to Include in Your Quote Request

Your approximate timeline ("looking to start in the next 4–6 weeks"), the scope of work as specifically as possible, the address or at least the neighbourhood, and whether you've already decided on materials or need guidance. The more specific you are, the faster contractors respond.

📞 Call vs. Text vs. Email

In Atlantic Canada's trades market, a phone call or text gets a response far faster than an email. Most busy contractors check their email infrequently. If you submitted a form online, follow up with a direct text or call 24 hours later if you haven't heard back.

Why 3 Quotes Always Saves You Money

The spread between the lowest and highest quote for the same job is typically 20–40% in Maritime Canada. For a $15,000 roofing job that's a $3,000–$6,000 difference for identical work. Getting only one quote is the single most expensive mistake homeowners make.

The middle quote is usually the right one. The lowest is sometimes a red flag (cut corners, unlicensed), and the highest is sometimes padded. Three quotes gives you a solid picture of fair market value for your specific job.

What a licensed plumber in Nova Scotia must carry, and how to verify credentials before they start work.

Plumbing is one of the most regulated trades in Nova Scotia — and for good reason. Bad plumbing work can cause water damage, sewage backups, or even health hazards. Before you hire anyone to touch your pipes in Halifax or Dartmouth, here's what you need to know.

Nova Scotia Plumbing Licensing

In Nova Scotia, anyone performing plumbing work must hold a valid journeyperson or master plumber certificate issued by the Nova Scotia Apprenticeship Agency. You can verify a plumber's license by contacting the agency directly or asking the contractor for their certificate number.

📋 What to Ask Every Plumber Before Booking

Ask for their NS plumber certificate number, proof of liability insurance (minimum $1 million), and confirmation that any required permits will be pulled for the job. In Halifax, most plumbing work beyond simple fixture replacements requires a permit from Halifax Regional Municipality.

🚨 Red Flags to Watch For

Unwillingness to pull permits, demanding full cash payment upfront, no written quote, no proof of insurance, or pressure to start immediately without any paperwork. Any of these should send you to the next plumber on your list.

EV Charger Installation in Halifax —
Cost & What to Expect

Everything you need to know about installing a Level 2 home EV charger in Nova Scotia — including permits and rebates.

EV ownership is growing fast in Atlantic Canada, and a home Level 2 charger is one of the best investments you can make alongside buying the car. Here's the full picture on getting one installed in Halifax or Dartmouth.

Level 1 vs Level 2 — What's the Difference?

Level 1 (Standard Outlet)

Plugs into a regular 120V outlet. Charges at 5–8 km of range per hour. Fine for plug-in hybrids — too slow for most EVs. No installation required but takes 40+ hours for a full charge.

Level 2 (240V — Recommended)

Requires a dedicated 240V circuit, like your dryer. Charges at 30–50 km of range per hour. Fully charges most EVs overnight. This is what most EV owners install at home.
